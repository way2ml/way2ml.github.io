# Louis de Broglie's hypothesis 1924

The motion of a particle is governed by the wave propagation properties of a 'pilot' wave called matter wave. The wavelength and the frequency of the pilot wave associated with a particle of momentum and energy are

λ=hp,ν=Eh\lambda = \frac{h}{p}, \nu = \frac{E}{h} λ=​p​​h​​,ν=​h​​E​​

He was rewarded the Nobel Prize in 1929 because of his Phd thesis.

By analogy to the other interference experiments, if this path differency is equal to an intergral number of wavelengths, the two beams will add constructively; that is, the radiation reflected by two adjacent layers of atoms will add constructively if

# The nature of wave function

Let the particle wave be represented by

ψ(r,t)=Asin(kx−ωt)\psi (r, t) = A \sin (kx - \omega t) ψ(r,t)=Asin(kx−ωt)

  1. The amplitude AAA is the same at all points in space.
  2. It has a well-defined wavelength, λ=2π/k\lambda = 2\pi/kλ=2π/k
  3. It has a well-defined frequency, ν=ω/2π\nu = \omega/2\piν=ω/2π
  4. It travels toward increasing values of xxx with a velocity

v=λν=ωkv = \lambda \nu = \frac{\omega}{k} v=λν=​k​​ω​​

Because the amplitude of the wave is the same for all values of xxx, the particle can be found with equal probability at any point in space, that is, the particle is completely unlocalized, Δx=∞\Delta x = \inftyΔx=∞. Actually, we should not be surprised by this result. A well-defined λ\lambdaλ implies a well-defined momentum for the particle(λ=h/p\lambda = h/pλ=h/p), that is, Δpx=0\Delta p_x = 0Δp​x​​=0. This inturn, according to the uncertainty principle, should lead to an infinite uncertainity in the coordinate of the particle. This particle is free particle with well-defined monentum and energy, the price paid is a complete lack of localization.

To describe a particle that is partially localized, we need a wave with an amplitude that is different from zero only over a small region of space where there is a chance of finding the particle. We need a wave that looks like the wave packet. The wavepacket can be written as

ψ(x,t)=∫0∞∫0∞A(k,ω)sin(kx−ωt)dkdω\psi(x, t)=\int_{0}^{\infty} \int_{0}^{\infty} A(k, \omega) \sin (k x-\omega t) d k d \omega ψ(x,t)=∫​0​∞​​∫​0​∞​​A(k,ω)sin(kx−ωt)dkdω

The coefficient AAA basically determines how much of a particular wavelength and frequency we mix with the others. The greater the range of kkk's (and therefore λ\lambdaλ's) that we mix, the narrower is the width of the resulting wave packet. This is why we cannot determine a component of the momentum of and coordinate simultaneously, no matter you measuring it or not.
