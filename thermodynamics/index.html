In thermodynamics, heat has a very specific meaning that is different from how we might use the word in everyday speech. Heat, with the symbol qqq or QQQ, is defined as thermal energy transferred between two systems at different temperatures that come in contact, and it has units of Joules(J).

TIP

Heat is sometimes called a process quantity, bacause it is defined in the context of a process by which energy can be transferred. We don't talk about a cup of coffee containing heat, but we can talk about the heat transferred from the cup of hot coffee to your hand.

When the two systems in contact are at the same temperature, we say they are in thermal equilibrium. The zeroth law of thermodynamics says that no heat is transferred between two objects in the thermal equilibrium, therefore they are the same temperature. Any time we use a thermometer, we are using the zeroth law of thermodynamics. In order to make sure the reading is accurate, we usually want to wait for the temperature reading to stay constant. WE are waiting for the themometer and the water to reach thermal equilibrium.

TIP

When a system absorbs or loses heat, the average kinetic energy of the molecules will change. Thus, heat transfer results in a change in the system's temperature as long as the system is not undergoing a phase change.

# Bond enthalpy and enthalpy of reaction

Chemical bonds represent potential energy. During chemical reactions, the bonds between atoms may break, reform or both to either absorb or release energy. The result is a change to the potential energy of the system. The heat absorbed or released from a system under constant pressure is known as enthalpy, and the change in enthalpy that results from a chemical reaction is the enthalpy of reaction. The enthalpy of reaction is often wrtten as ΔHrxn\Delta H_\text{rxn}ΔH​rxn​​.

As a rule, breaking bonds between aatoms requires adding energy. The stronger the bond, the more energy it takes to break the bond. The opposite process of forming new bonds always releases energy. The stronger the bond fromed, the omre energy is released during the bond formation process.

Mathematically, we can think of the enthalpy of reaction as the difference between the potential energy from the product and the potential energy of the reactant bonds:

ΔHrxn=potentialenergyofproductbonds−potentialenergyofreactantbonds\Delta H_\text{rxn} = \text{potential energy of product bonds} - \text{potential energy of reactant bonds} ΔH​rxn​​=potentialenergyofproductbonds−potentialenergyofreactantbonds

=energyaddedtobreakreactantbonds+energyreleasedwhenmakingproductbonds= \text{energy added to break reactant bonds} + \text{energy released when making product bonds} =energyaddedtobreakreactantbonds+energyreleasedwhenmakingproductbonds

Bond enthalpy (which is also known as bond-dissociation enthalpy, average bond energy, or bond strength) is the energy to break 1 mol of a bond in the gas phase.

  • Always positive (need energy to break bonds)
  • Reverse = making a bond = always negative (release energy)

Reactions where the products have a lower potential energy than the reactants are exothermic. Reactions where the products have a higher potential energy than the reactants are endothermic. In a exothermic reaction, the released energy doesn't simply disappear. Instead it is converted to kinetic energy, which produces heat. This is observed as an increase in temperature as the reaction progresses.
